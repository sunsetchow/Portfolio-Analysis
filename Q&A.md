**ASSET ALLOCATION**

**Q: Why 50% equity for Andrea? Isn't that aggressive for someone with $306K annual expenses and a $50M trust to draw from?**

A: Andrea's Swiss residency actually supports higher equity allocation. Capital gains aren't taxed in Switzerland, so equity appreciation is the most tax-efficient way to grow wealth. Meanwhile, her Swiss trust provides a reliable income backstop of $1M annually, which comfortably covers expenses. The 50% in diversifiers — bonds, gold, real estate, cash — provides downside protection without sacrificing the tax advantage.

**Q: Why high-dividend equities for Francis rather than growth equities?**

A: Francis has an immediate cash flow problem — expenses of $415K against only $229K in income. High-dividend equities serve a dual purpose: they generate the cash flow he needs to close that gap, and they provide diversification away from the PE fund's growth-oriented risk profile. Once liquidity stabilizes post-PE exit, we can reassess the growth-vs-income mix.

**Q: Why no alternative investments like hedge funds or private credit?**

A: Simplicity and liquidity are priorities here. Francis already has significant illiquid exposure through the PE fund. Adding more alternatives would compound that problem. For Andrea, the Swiss wealth tax applies to net assets regardless of liquidity, so adding illiquid alternatives creates tax drag without a liquidity benefit. We'd rather keep the portfolios in transparent, liquid instruments that are easy to rebalance and report across two jurisdictions.

**Q: Why gold in both Andrea's and Francis's portfolios?**

A: Gold serves as a non-correlated hedge — it doesn't generate taxable income, which benefits both tax regimes. For Andrea, it's wealth tax-neutral relative to equities but provides crisis protection. For Francis, it's a store of value that doesn't trigger capital gains until sold, complementing the deviation-based rebalancing strategy.

**CHILDREN'S TRUST**

**Q: Why not start the trust equity-heavy from day one given the long time horizon?**

A: Because the trust has a near-term cash obligation — funding children's expenses before graduation. If we go equity-heavy and hit a market downturn in years one or two, we'd be forced to sell at a loss to meet those expenses. The bond-heavy start ensures we can meet income distribution requirements without being a forced seller. Once those obligations roll off, we shift to growth.

**Q: Why a $300K annual dividend from the trust? How did you arrive at that number?**

A: It's calibrated to the combined children's expenses — $100K for Brian with Andrea and $75K each for Karen, Betsy, and Thatcher with Francis, totaling roughly $325K. The $300K distribution covers the majority while preserving capital in the trust for long-term compounding. The slight shortfall is absorbed by each parent's personal cash flow.

**Q: How is the $300K distributed between Andrea and Francis?**

A: Pro-rata by number of dependents. Andrea has one child, Francis has three, so the split is 25/75 — roughly $75K to Andrea and $225K to Francis. This directly offsets each parent's child-related expenses proportionally.

**Q: What happens to the trust after all children graduate?**

A: The $300K distribution stops and the full portfolio return compounds within the trust. At that point, the allocation is already shifted toward growth equity, so it becomes a pure long-term accumulation vehicle for the next generation.

**RISK & RETURN**

**Q: Your Sharpe ratios range from 0.40 to 0.59 — aren't those modest?**

A: They're realistic. These are after accounting for the constraints each portfolio faces — Andrea's wealth tax drag, Francis's PE illiquidity, and the trust's income distribution requirement. A 0.59 Sharpe for Francis post-diversification is actually quite strong given the starting point of near-total PE concentration. We'd rather present achievable numbers than overstate returns.

**Q: What return assumptions are you using? Are these nominal or real?**

A: These are nominal returns based on long-term historical asset class averages — US equity at roughly 10-11%, global equity slightly lower, bonds at 4-5%, gold at 7-8%. We're not assuming any alpha from active management. The portfolio-level returns are weighted averages of these inputs.

**Q: How do you handle currency risk for Andrea given she's earning and spending in Swiss francs but invested in US equities?**

A: Good question. Andrea's Swiss trust income is in CHF, and her living expenses are in CHF. The US and global equity exposure does introduce currency risk. In practice, we'd consider selective FX hedging on the bond allocation where returns are lower and currency moves can easily wipe out yield. For equities, we'd leave the exposure unhedged — over a 10-year horizon, currency effects tend to wash out, and hedging costs would reduce returns.

**REBALANCING & TAX**

**Q: You mention deviation-based rebalancing for Francis — what's the threshold?**

A: We'd set bands of roughly 5 percentage points around target weights. Rebalancing is only triggered when an asset class drifts beyond that band, rather than on a calendar basis. This minimizes the number of taxable events while keeping the portfolio within acceptable risk parameters.

**Q: How does Andrea's semi-annual rebalancing interact with Swiss wealth tax timing?**

A: Swiss wealth tax is assessed on December 31st net asset values in most cantons. We'd time the year-end rebalance to optimize the portfolio's reported composition on that date — for example, shifting temporarily toward lower-valued or exempt asset classes if structurally appropriate. This is a standard Swiss tax planning technique.

**FEES & IMPLEMENTATION**

**Q: The 0.5% fee excludes the PE fund — doesn't that create a misaligned incentive where you'd prefer Francis to exit PE faster?**

A: Fair challenge. We excluded PE because we have no direct management influence over an illiquid fund with its own GP. Charging on assets we can't actively manage would be value-destructive for the client. The PE exit timeline is driven by the fund's lifecycle, not our fee structure. Once proceeds are reinvested into liquid assets, they fall under our management and fee basis — so our incentive is to deploy those proceeds well, not to accelerate exit.
